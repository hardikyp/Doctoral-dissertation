\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chpt-introduction}
Why static structures are not enough. Key ideas to include:
\begin{itemize} 
    \item Static systems are excellent at carrying loads efficiently, but they are limited in adaptability.
	\item Many applications benefit from systems that can change configuration, geometry, stiffness, or mechanical response.
	\item Reconfigurability enables deployability, compact transport, multi-functionality, adaptability to boundary conditions, energy absorption, controlled collapse, tunable response, and shape morphing.
	\item This creates a major design challenge: how do we endow structural systems with motion and function without abandoning structural usefulness?
\end{itemize}
    
End this section with a pivot like: The challenge is therefore not merely to create moving systems, but to transform structurally meaningful static topologies into reconfigurable systems that can achieve controlled motion and useful mechanical function.

\section{Problem statement}\label{01-sec-problem-statement}
The dissertation is about a systematic transformation from static structural topology to reconfigurable system to functional behavior.

Focus is on identifying ways to start with established structural topologies and transform them into systems capable of controlled reconfiguration while preserving meaningful structural behavior. Not on inventing new moving structures.

This dissertation asks how static structural topologies can be systematically transformed into reconfigurable systems that retain structural relevance while gaining controlled mobility and functional performance.

\section{Research objectives}\label{01-sec-objectives}
\begin{itemize}
\item Develop a systematic framework for transforming static truss topologies into reconfigurable structural systems.
\item Establish computational and mechanical tools for analyzing reconfigurable systems with joint compliance and large-displacement response.
\item Demonstrate how reconfigurable structural systems can be designed for functional behaviors such as deployment, energy absorption, and controlled motion.
\item Extend the broader static-to-reconfigurable viewpoint to shape-morphing structural forms, including curved-origami-inspired hull systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope of the thesis}\label{01-sec-scope}
This dissertation aims at incorporating existing means of reconfigurability in existing structures and is organized as follows:

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-reconfigurable-trusses}} introduces a method for transforming static trusses into reconfigurable systems. This method uses Grahsof's flat-foldability criterion for planar quadrilateral linkages to strategically introduce a new node in every triangular unit of a given truss. These added nodes convert triangular units into flat-foldable quadrilateral linkages, giving the truss kinematic degrees of freedom for global reconfigurability. The chapter presents a general workflow for applying these transformations to most single-chorded truss designs and supports it with a sequential kinematic analysis framework to study deployment behavior of the resulting systems. This workflow is first demonstrated on traditional truss designs and then extended to topology-optimized trusses with arbitrary geometries, to showcase its broad applicability. The chapter also presents experimental structural analysis of proof-of-concept prototypes to demonstrate fabrication feasibility and show that the transformed systems preserve design load-bearing capacity and structural stiffness.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-torsional-springs}} explores ways to introduce rotation stiffness in the folding joints of the reconfigurable trusses from Chatper~\ref{chpt-reconfigurable-trusses}. We introduce a Three-node Torsional Spring (3NTS) element the develops the mechanics formulation needed to model compliant reconfigurable systems, with emphasis on torsional spring behavior and nonlinear structural analysis. The chapter introduces a first-principles derivation of a torsional spring element and impliments it in a nonlinear structural solver. This spring model is validated against the analytical solution of two benchmark examples. The chapter then discusses how these springs can be introduced in the reconfigurable trusses to obtain functional benifits like safeguards agaist  load-reversal, controlled path-dependent deployment and absorption of energy from external loads. 

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-functional-applications}} builds on the 3NTS formulation from Chatper~\ref{chpt-torsional-springs} to design bi- and multi-stable torsional springs. The reconfigurable truss systems augmented with springs explores how reconfigurable truss systems can be designed for function, including energy absorption, controlled deployment, and path-governed response.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-origami-hulls}} broadens the dissertation's perspective to shape-morphing structural systems and explores the use of curved-origami principles for the design of high-speed ship hulls.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-conclusion}} summarizes the main contributions, discusses limitations and outlines the scope of future research directions.

Two appendices are also included to provide important context for explaining the ideas and methods developed in the bulk of the dissertation. These appendices act as a background information for theories used in kinematic and hydrodynamic analysis tools in this dissertation.

\textbf{Appendix~\ref{apdx-sequential-kinematics}} describes the mathematics behind positional analysis of planar quadrilateral linkages which forms the backbone of the sequential kinematic analysis framework for the reconfigurable trusses. This appendix also covers positional analysis of the special 6-bar linkage case discussed in Chatper~\ref{chpt-reconfigurable-trusses} as well as a note on interpreting the percentage of actuation of each kinematic degree of freedom in the reconfigurable trusses.

\textbf{Appendix~\ref{apdx-powersea-theory}} describes the theoretical framework of the Powersea software used for the hydrodynamic analyses of planing hulls in Chatper~\ref{chpt-origami-hulls}. This appendix briefly outlines the \textit{low aspect-ratio strip theory} for hull hydrodynamics, states the key assumptions and explains why it ends up being a suitable tool for doing comparative hydrodynamic studies between curved-crease origami and conventional planing hulls.
