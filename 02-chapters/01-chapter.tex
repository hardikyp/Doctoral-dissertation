\chapter{Introduction}
\label{chpt-introduction}
Why static structures are not enough. Key ideas to include:
\begin{itemize} 
    \item Static systems are excellent at carrying loads efficiently, but they are limited in adaptability.
	\item Many applications benefit from systems that can change configuration, geometry, stiffness, or mechanical response.
	\item Reconfigurability enables deployability, compact transport, multi-functionality, adaptability to boundary conditions, energy absorption, controlled collapse, tunable response, and shape morphing.
	\item This creates a major design challenge: how do we endow structural systems with motion and function without abandoning structural usefulness?
\end{itemize}
    
End this section with a pivot like: The challenge is therefore not merely to create moving systems, but to transform structurally meaningful static topologies into reconfigurable systems that can achieve controlled motion and useful mechanical function.

\section{Problem statement}\label{01-sec-problem-statement}
The dissertation is about a systematic transformation from static structural topology to reconfigurable system to functional behavior.

Focus is on identifying ways to start with established structural topologies and transform them into systems capable of controlled reconfiguration while preserving meaningful structural behavior. Not on inventing new moving structures.

This dissertation asks how static structural topologies can be systematically transformed into reconfigurable systems that retain structural relevance while gaining controlled mobility and functional performance.

\section{Research objectives}\label{01-sec-objectives}
\begin{itemize}
\item Develop a systematic framework for transforming static truss topologies into reconfigurable structural systems.
\item Establish computational and mechanical tools for analyzing reconfigurable systems with joint compliance and large-displacement response.
\item Demonstrate how reconfigurable structural systems can be designed for functional behaviors such as deployment, energy absorption, and controlled motion.
\item Extend the broader static-to-reconfigurable viewpoint to shape-morphing structural forms, including curved-origami-inspired hull systems.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope of the thesis}\label{01-sec-scope}


\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-reconfigurable-trusses}} presents the framework for transforming static truss topologies into reconfigurable systems and demonstrates the approach through representative examples, fabrication strategies, and experimental validation.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-torsional-springs}} develops the mechanics formulation needed to model compliant reconfigurable systems, with emphasis on torsional spring behavior and nonlinear structural analysis.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-functional-applications}} explores how reconfigurable truss systems can be designed for function, including energy absorption, controlled deployment, and path-governed response.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-origami-hulls}} broadens the dissertation perspective to shape-morphing structural systems and examines curved-origami-based hulls as a complementary manifestation of structural reconfigurability.

\textbf{Chatper~\ref{chpt-conclusion}} summarizes the main findings and discusses the limitations of the work presented in this thesis. It also outlines future research directions.
